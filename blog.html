2026-06-28 Plastics

The cleanest plastic you've never heard of

Every finished model kit leaves behind a plastic skeleton. It turns out to be some of the best recycling feedstock we can get our hands on.

If you have ever built a Gunpla kit, you know the ritual: clip each part from the frame, and set the empty frame aside. That frame — the runner, with its sprues — is pure, moulded polystyrene, sometimes ABS. It has never held food. It has never been outdoors. It was manufactured to a tight spec and it is already sorted by colour on the frame.

Compare that to a curbside plastic bin, where mixed resins, food residue, and film all arrive tangled together. Sorting and cleaning that mess is most of what makes recycling expensive and unreliable. Model-kit runners skip the hardest steps entirely.

Clean, uniform, single-resin, pre-sorted — a recycler's idea of a perfect input.

The catch is volume: no single builder produces much. That is exactly why we collect through hobby stores. A shop concentrates the output of hundreds of builders into one bin, and many of them already hoard their runners out of a vague sense that the plastic is too nice to bin. It is. We shred it, sort by colour, dry it, and feed it straight into injection molding.

2026-06-12 Project HEX

A battery goldmine inside disposable vapes

Most disposable vapes are thrown away with a perfectly good lithium cell still inside. Multiply that by how many are sold, and the waste is staggering.

A disposable vape is, functionally, a lithium cell wrapped in plastic and marketed as trash. The cell is often rated for hundreds of charge cycles; the device is designed to be used until the liquid runs out — typically a handful of days — and then discarded. The battery inside is usually still healthy.

Thrown in the bin, that cell becomes a hazard: crushed in a truck or a sorting facility, lithium cells start fires. Recovered properly, it becomes a resource. Project HEX exists to do the second thing.

We collect spent disposables through vape retailers, pull the cells, and test and grade each one. Cells that pass go into our hexagonal power units — solar-topped, USB-C, magnetically tiling into larger arrays. Cells that fail are routed to proper battery recycling. Nothing goes back in the bin.

2026-05-30 Process

Meeting the material where it lands

The hardest part of recycling is not processing the material. It is collecting it cleanly in the first place.

Ask people to sort, store, and transport their own waste to a facility and most of it never arrives — or arrives contaminated. So we inverted the problem. Instead of building a destination, we partner with the businesses the material already passes through and put a bin right there.

A hobby store's customers generate runners. A repair shop accumulates pulled cells. A vape retailer collects spent disposables. An electronics retailer like Best Buy already runs take-back. In every case the material returns to roughly where it was sold — so collection is convenient, local, and pre-sorted by the nature of the partner.

It keeps our loop short and our feedstock clean, and it gives each partner a visible way to stand behind what they sell. If you run a business that fits one of these streams, we would like to hear from you.
