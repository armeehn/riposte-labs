Recycling 101

A practical guide to every major material stream: what belongs in the bin, what doesn't, and why. Rules vary by municipality; treat this page as the fundamentals and your local program as the final word.

SEC.01

The Basics

// how recycling actually works

Recycling is a manufacturing supply chain, not a magic bin.

Whatever you put in the bin gets collected, sorted at a materials recovery facility (MRF), baled, and sold as feedstock to manufacturers. If a bale is contaminated (food residue, wrong materials, tangled items), its value drops and it may be landfilled anyway.

That's why the rules matter: clean, empty, dry, and loose. One greasy container can spoil an entire bale of paper. Plastic bags jam sorting machinery. When you recycle well, the material actually gets a second life; when you guess, it often doesn't.

The scale of the gap is large: Canada discarded 5,152 kt of plastic in 2022 and reprocessed only about 6.5% of it into new pellets, flakes, and fibres.1

Local rules winEvery municipality accepts a different set of materials. Check your local program's list once; it takes five minutes and beats years of guessing.
SEC.02

Plastics

// know your resin codes

The number in the triangle is a resin code, not a promise of recyclability.

Only some resins have strong recycling markets. Bottles and jugs (#1, #2) are the reliable ones; most programs also take #5 tubs. Film, bags, and foam almost never belong in a curbside bin.

1PETbottles, jars: widely accepted
2HDPEjugs, tubs: widely accepted
3PVCpipes, blister packs: rarely accepted
4LDPEfilms, bags: store drop-off only
5PPtubs, caps: accepted in many programs
6PSfoam, cutlery: rarely accepted
7Othermixed resins: generally not accepted
Bin: usually yes
  • Beverage + food bottles and jars (#1)
  • Milk jugs, detergent bottles (#2)
  • Yogurt tubs, takeout containers (#5, check locally)
  • Rinsed, with lids on (most programs)
Bin: no
  • Plastic bags + film (jams sorters; use store drop-off)
  • Foam / polystyrene of any kind
  • Straws, cutlery, and anything smaller than a credit card
  • "Compostable" bioplastics (different stream entirely)
The Riposte anglePolystyrene (#6) and ABS (#7), the resins in model kits, have no BC buyer that pays, and no BC program takes plastics from business at all.2 That clean, single-resin material is exactly what our local-loop injection molding program exists for: we take it and mold it into long-lived products.
SEC.03

Paper + Cardboard

// the fibre stream

Paper fibre can be recycled five to seven times before it wears out, provided it stays clean and dry.

Cardboard is one of the most valuable curbside materials. Flatten boxes so they sort correctly and take less space. Wet or food-soiled fibre can't be recycled: grease physically ruins the pulping process.

Bin: yes
  • Flattened cardboard boxes
  • Office paper, mail, newspaper, magazines
  • Paperboard (cereal boxes, shoe boxes)
  • Clean pizza-box lids (tear off the greasy half)
Bin: no
  • Grease- or food-soiled paper
  • Used paper towels, napkins, tissues
  • Wax- or plastic-coated cups (most programs)
  • Loose shredded paper (bags of it jam sorters; check locally)
SEC.04

Glass

// infinitely recyclable

Container glass can be melted and remade forever with no loss of quality.

The catch: only container glass. Window panes, drinking glasses, ceramics, and heat-treated glass (like bakeware) have different melting points and ruin batches of new containers. Some municipalities collect glass separately or not at all; check yours.

Bin: yes
  • Bottles: any colour, rinsed
  • Food jars, lids removed (recycle metal lids with metals)
Bin: no
  • Window or mirror glass
  • Drinking glasses, ceramics, porcelain
  • Heat-resistant bakeware
  • Light bulbs (CFLs are hazardous-waste drop-off)
SEC.05

Metals

// the highest-value stream

Recycling aluminum uses roughly 95% less energy than producing it from ore. It is the recycling success story.

Aluminum and steel cans can be recycled indefinitely. Scrap metal (pans, tools, wire) is valuable too, but it belongs at a scrap yard or metal drop-off, not in the curbside bin where it damages equipment.

Bin: yes
  • Aluminum cans, rinsed
  • Steel / tin food cans
  • Clean aluminum foil, balled up (check locally)
  • Empty aerosol cans (most programs)
Bin: no
  • Scrap metal, tools, cookware (take to scrap yard)
  • Wire, cables, hangers (tanglers)
  • Partially full aerosol or propane cans (hazardous)
SEC.06

Batteries + E-Waste

// never in the bin. ever.

Batteries do not go in any curbside bin, recycling or trash. Crushed lithium cells start fires in trucks and facilities.

Every battery type has a proper drop-off route: household battery collection points, hazardous-waste facilities, and retailer take-back programs. Electronics carry both recoverable metals and hazardous components, so they belong in dedicated e-waste programs.

Disposable vapes are the worst offender: a lithium cell, sealed in plastic, marketed as trash. Most are discarded with the cell still healthy. Embedded cells also make them a fire vector: discarded batteries drove 448 waste-facility fires across the US and Canada in 2025, with vapes a named driver.3 This is the waste stream Project HEX exists to intercept: we collect them, reclaim the cells, and build them into modular solar-topped power units.

Take-back is still patchy in Canada. Quebec runs the only operating vape program (launched 2025); BC has none today, but a producer-funded program is legislated for October 2027.4 Until then, a household battery drop-off or a specialist collector is the only compliant route.

Proper routes
  • Household batteries → collection points (many retailers)
  • Lithium / rechargeable → battery take-back programs
  • Phones, laptops, cables → e-waste drop-off
  • Disposable vapes → vape take-back, or send them to us
Never
  • Any battery in curbside recycling
  • Any battery in household trash
  • Electronics in curbside recycling
  • Tape-free loose lithium cells (tape the terminals)
Collecting nowRiposte Laboratories accepts dead disposable vapes for cell reclamation. Contact us to arrange a drop-off.
SEC.07

Organics

// food + yard waste

Food waste in a landfill decomposes without oxygen and produces methane, a potent greenhouse gas. Composted, it becomes soil.

If your area has green-bin collection, use it for food scraps and yard trimmings. If not, backyard composting handles fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells, and leaves. Keep meat, dairy, and oils out of home compost; they attract pests and break down poorly.

Compost: yes
  • Fruit + vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells
  • Yard trimmings, leaves, plain paper (home compost)
  • Meat + dairy in municipal green bins (check locally)
Compost: no
  • Plastic of any kind, including "compostable" bioplastic at home
  • Meat, dairy, oils in backyard piles
  • Pet waste (pathogens)
SEC.08

Textiles

// the forgotten stream

Clothing never belongs in the curbside bin, but almost all of it can be reused or recycled through the right channel.

Wearable clothing should be donated or resold. Damaged textiles still have value: many donation centers and dedicated textile-recycling programs accept worn-out items and turn them into insulation, rags, and fibre fill. Landfilling clothing wastes both the material and everything it took to make it.

Proper routes
  • Wearable → donation, resale, repair
  • Damaged → textile-recycling drop-off (label items "rags")
  • Shoes, linens, curtains → most textile programs take them
Never
  • Any textiles in curbside recycling (tanglers)
  • Wet or moldy textiles in donation bins
SEC.09

Golden Rules

// if you remember nothing else

Five rules cover 95% of good recycling.

01Clean, empty, dry

Rinse containers. One saucy jar can contaminate a whole bale.

02Never bag recyclables

Put items in the bin loose. Bagged loads get landfilled unopened.

03No tanglers

Hoses, cords, wire, textiles, chains: they shut down sorting lines.

04Batteries never bin

Not recycling, not trash. Drop-off only. Fires are not hypothetical.

05When in doubt, find out

Wishcycling does more harm than landfilling. Check your local list.
